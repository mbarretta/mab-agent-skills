# Framework Reference

This document describes the theoretical foundations underlying the Life Goals system. Read the relevant section when you need to go deeper on a methodology during a session.

---

## How the Frameworks Interlock

The system layers four frameworks, each operating at a different level:

```
LEVEL 1 — VISION & MEANING
  GTD Horizons (purpose → projects)
  ↓
LEVEL 2 — GOAL STRUCTURE
  OKRs (objectives → measurable outcomes)
  ↓
LEVEL 3 — EXECUTION RHYTHM
  12 Week Year (90-day urgency + weekly scorecards)
  ↓
LEVEL 4 — DAILY BEHAVIOR
  Atomic Habits (identity → habit loops)
```

No single framework handles all levels well. GTD is great for clarity and capture but weak on motivation. OKRs create measurable structure but don't tell you *how* to show up every day. Atomic Habits is deeply practical but needs a larger vision to point toward. The 12 Week Year creates the urgency to actually execute. Together, they form a complete system.

## GTD Horizons Model (David Allen)

The core insight: most productivity systems work at the task level ("what do I do next?") without ever connecting to why it matters. The Horizons model provides the altitude framework.

### The Six Horizons

| Horizon | Altitude | Time Frame | Question |
|---------|----------|------------|----------|
| Life Purpose & Values | 50,000 ft | 20-30+ years | Why am I here? What do I stand for? |
| Long-term Vision | 40,000 ft | 10+ years | What does my ideal life look like? |
| Goals | 30,000 ft | 1-3 years | What do I want to achieve? |
| Areas of Focus | 20,000 ft | Ongoing | What must I maintain to function? |
| Projects | 10,000 ft | Weeks-months | What outcomes am I committed to? |
| Next Actions | Runway | Today-this week | What do I actually do next? |

### How This Maps to the Life Goals System

- Horizons 6-5 → North Star Statement + 10-Year Milestones
- Horizon 4 → Annual OKRs
- Horizon 3 → 3-Year Goals  
- Horizon 2 → 90-Day Sprint goals and projects
- Horizon 1 → Weekly actions and habits

### The Weekly Review (GTD)

Allen's most enduring contribution. The Weekly Review is a ritual that keeps all horizons in sync. Without it, the runway (daily tasks) disconnects from the purpose (why you're doing any of it). Key steps:
1. Capture and process all loose ends (notes, ideas, commitments)
2. Review each active project for stuck items or next actions
3. Scan the calendar 1-2 weeks ahead
4. Review goals at the 30k ft level — are current projects serving them?
5. Identify the 1-3 most important outcomes for the coming week

## OKRs (John Doerr, *Measure What Matters*)

OKRs bring the rigor of measurability to aspirational goals without sacrificing ambition. Originally developed at Intel and popularized at Google.

### Structure

**Objective** — Qualitative, aspirational, motivating. Answers "where do I want to go?" Should be slightly uncomfortable — if it's obviously achievable, it's not ambitious enough.

**Key Results** (2-4 per objective) — Quantitative, time-bound, binary-ish. Answers "how will I know I got there?" Good KRs:
- Are measurable (a number, not a yes/no)
- Are outcomes, not activities ("revenue of $X" not "make 50 sales calls")
- Would be hard to game (achieving a KR should mean the objective actually advanced)
- Set at ~70% confidence — if you're 100% confident, the bar is too low

### Scoring

At the end of the period, score each KR 0.0-1.0. A score of 0.7 is a "good" result on an ambitious KR. Consistently scoring 1.0 means your KRs aren't ambitious enough.

### Quarterly vs. Annual OKRs

Annual OKRs: directional, tied to 3-year goals. Monthly or quarterly: operational, tied to the current sprint. In this system, Annual OKRs live at the 1-year horizon and 90-day sprint goals function as OKRs at the quarterly level.

### OKRs for Personal Life

OKRs were designed for organizations but adapt well personally with some adjustments:
- Fewer objectives (3-4 max for an individual vs. 5-7 for a team)
- Key Results can be "outcome by date" rather than strictly numerical
- Score honestly — this is about your own clarity, not performance management

## Atomic Habits (James Clear)

The deepest insight: behavior change that lasts almost always flows from *identity* change, not outcome-chasing. "I want to run a marathon" fails when motivation dips. "I am a runner" survives a bad week.

### The 4 Laws of Behavior Change

Every habit is built through a feedback loop: **Cue → Craving → Response → Reward**

To build a habit, make each step easier:

| Law | Direction | How |
|-----|-----------|-----|
| 1. Make it Obvious | Cue | Implementation intentions, environment design, habit stacking |
| 2. Make it Attractive | Craving | Temptation bundling, social environment, reframing |
| 3. Make it Easy | Response | Reduce friction, 2-minute rule, prime the environment |
| 4. Make it Satisfying | Reward | Immediate reward, habit tracking, never miss twice |

To break a bad habit, invert each law: make it invisible, unattractive, hard, and unsatisfying.

### Implementation Intentions

The research is clear: specifying *when* and *where* dramatically increases follow-through. The formula:

> "I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION]."

Not "I'll exercise more" → "I will do 20 minutes of strength training at 6:30am in my garage."

### Habit Stacking

Attach new habits to existing ones. The formula:

> "After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]."

Examples:
- "After I pour my morning coffee, I will review my top priority for the day."
- "After I sit down at my desk, I will spend 5 minutes reviewing my goals."
- "After dinner, I will walk for 15 minutes before doing anything else."

The existing habit acts as a reliable trigger — it already happens every day, no willpower required.

### The 2-Minute Rule

Scale any habit down to 2 minutes to start. "Read before bed" → "Read one page before bed." "Exercise daily" → "Put on workout clothes." The point isn't to do a tiny habit forever — it's to eliminate resistance and build the identity of someone who shows up. You can always extend after the 2-minute anchor is solid.

### Never Miss Twice

Missing once is an accident. Missing twice is the start of a new (bad) habit. The goal isn't perfection — it's recovery. When life disrupts a habit, the only rule that matters is: don't miss two in a row. Get back on track immediately.

### Identity-Based Habit Design

The most powerful question in habit design: "What would a person who [has this goal] do?" Then ask: "What would a person who *is* [that identity] do?" Small actions accumulate into evidence. The evidence becomes belief. The belief becomes identity. The identity sustains behavior.

Examples:
- "I am someone who protects their health." Every workout is a vote for that identity.
- "I am a disciplined saver." Every transfer to retirement is a vote.
- "I am someone who shows up for my family." Every protected evening is a vote.

## The 12 Week Year (Brian Moran)

The central insight: annual planning fails because a full year doesn't create enough urgency. The further away a deadline, the easier it is to procrastinate. The 12 Week Year treats each 90-day period as a complete "year" — with the full urgency, focus, and retrospective cycle compressed into 12 weeks.

### Why 12 Weeks

- Long enough to make real progress on meaningful goals
- Short enough to feel the deadline pressure throughout
- Forces ruthless prioritization: you can only accomplish so much in 12 weeks, which means choosing what actually matters
- Quarterly reviews become full retrospectives, not check-ins

### Sprint Design

**Theme**: One phrase that captures the 12-week focus. Makes it memorable and helps you stay oriented.

**Sprint goals**: 2-4 goals, each with a clear metric and target. More than 4 dilutes attention. Every goal should meaningfully advance an annual OKR.

**Weekly scorecard**: Each goal gets a weekly score (on-track / at-risk / off-track). Scoring weekly makes problems visible early — not in week 11.

**The model week**: Map your ideal week in advance. Identify the specific times for high-priority habit execution. A planned week beats an improvised one.

### Execution Rhythm

**Weekly planning session** (~20-30 min, Sunday or Monday morning):
1. Review sprint goals and current scores
2. Identify the 3-5 most important actions for this week
3. Block time for critical habits and deep work
4. Brief personal retrospective: what went well, what to adjust

**Weekly accountability**: Optional but powerful — even a brief check-in with a partner about goals creates social commitment. Research shows accountability dramatically increases follow-through on hard goals.

### Sprint Retrospective

At the end of each 12-week sprint:
1. Score each goal: what % of the target was achieved?
2. Identify the top 1-2 reasons for success (to repeat) and failure (to fix)
3. Decide: continue this goal into the next sprint, complete, or retire it
4. Design the next sprint — new theme, new goals, updated habits

## The Wheel of Life

A simple but useful diagnostic tool. Draw a wheel with 6-8 spokes, each representing a life domain. Score each domain 1-10 (distance from center). The resulting shape shows:
- Where life feels full and satisfying
- Where there's a significant gap between current state and desired state
- How balanced or imbalanced things feel overall

Use it at the start of each annual review and optionally quarterly. The goal isn't a perfectly round wheel — different seasons call for different emphases. But a very lopsided wheel sustained for years usually indicates something important being neglected.

## Retirement and Financial Planning Integration

For the financial domain specifically, goal-setting needs to connect to the numbers. Key frameworks:

**The 4% Rule**: A common retirement planning heuristic — to sustain withdrawals indefinitely, you need a portfolio of approximately 25x your annual expenses. (This assumes a ~4% annual withdrawal rate with a balanced portfolio.) This gives a concrete retirement number: Annual expenses × 25.

**Retirement readiness stages**:
- **Accumulation** (working years): Maximize savings rate, invest aggressively, minimize lifestyle inflation
- **Pre-retirement** (5-10 years out): Shift toward capital preservation, reduce debt, solidify income plan
- **Transition**: Social Security optimization, healthcare bridge, portfolio drawdown strategy

**The savings rate lever**: Of all the variables in retirement planning (returns, time horizon, expenses), savings rate is the one you most directly control. Even modest increases in savings rate compound dramatically over a decade.

**For families with dependents**: Layer in:
- Emergency fund (6-12 months expenses, given family size)
- Term life insurance coverage while dependents are still dependent
- College planning (529s) if applicable
- Estate planning basics (will, beneficiary designations, power of attorney)

**The key integration with goals**: Financial goals need to be *specific* to work. "Save more" fails. "Reach $X in retirement accounts by [YEAR], contributing $Y/month starting [DATE]" is a plan.
