# Life Domains Reference

This file provides depth for each of the six life domains — guiding questions for initial setup, common patterns and traps, and domain-specific goal-setting guidance.

---

## 🏥 Health & Longevity

**Why this domain anchors the others**: Without physical and mental energy, every other domain suffers. Career goals require cognitive capacity. Family requires presence. Financial goals require decades of earning. Health is often the domain people plan last — and regret neglecting first.

**Core dimensions**:
- Physical fitness and strength
- Cardiovascular health and longevity markers
- Sleep quality and quantity
- Mental/emotional health and stress management
- Energy and vitality day-to-day
- Preventive care and medical relationships

### Guiding Questions

**Current state**: "How would you describe your energy and physical condition right now? What's working, what's not?"

**Vision**: "At 75 or 80, what do you want your physical life to look like? What do you want to still be able to do?"

**Gaps**: "What's the biggest health-related thing you know you should be doing but aren't? What's in the way?"

**Habits**: "What's one health habit that, if you did it consistently for the next year, would have the most impact?"

### Common Patterns and Traps

**The 'I'll start when...' trap**: Many people have health goals that are perpetually contingent ("when work calms down," "after the holidays"). Health goals are worth treating as non-negotiable rather than context-dependent.

**The acute vs. chronic gap**: People respond urgently to acute health events (a bad test result, an injury) but neglect chronic risks (stress accumulation, poor sleep, gradual weight gain). Goal systems need to make chronic health visible.

**Mental health often gets omitted**: In initial goal scans, people frequently focus on physical health and skip mental/emotional health entirely. Prompt specifically for this: sleep quality, stress levels, anxiety or depression patterns, emotional resilience.

### Domain-Specific Goal Examples

- 10-year: "In strong physical shape for an active retirement; no major preventable chronic conditions"
- 3-year OKR Objective: "Build a durable fitness foundation and resolve chronic back pain"
- Annual KR: "Consistent 4x/week strength training from Q1 through Q4, measured by weekly logs"
- Habit: "After morning coffee, I put on workout clothes before opening any screens" (identity: "I am someone who protects my health")

## 💰 Financial & Retirement

**Why this domain requires extra specificity**: Financial goals fail more often due to vagueness than discipline. "Save more" fails. "Contribute $1,500/month to my 401k starting May 1" succeeds. This domain needs numbers.

**Core dimensions**:
- Retirement savings and investment portfolio
- Family financial security (emergency fund, insurance, estate planning)
- Debt management and elimination
- Income growth and diversification
- College savings (if applicable)
- Day-to-day cash flow and spending

### Guiding Questions

**Current state**: "Where are you with retirement savings right now — do you have a rough sense of how far along you are toward where you'd need to be?"

**Retirement target**: "What does retirement look like for you? At what age? What kind of lifestyle? Do you want to fully stop working or do something different?"

**Family security**: "If something happened to you tomorrow, is your family financially protected? Life insurance, will, beneficiary designations — how current are those?"

**Stress points**: "What's the biggest financial stressor for you right now? What keeps you up at night?"

**Vision**: "What does financial freedom feel like to you? What would you do differently if money wasn't the constraint?"

### The Retirement Math (Make It Concrete)

For someone at 48 wanting to retire around 62-65, the key variables:

1. **Annual retirement expenses**: What will you spend per year in retirement? (Often estimated at 70-80% of pre-retirement income for a comfortable lifestyle, or calculate bottom-up from actual expected costs)

2. **Portfolio target**: Annual expenses × 25 (using the 4% rule). Example: $120k/year expenses = $3M target

3. **Current portfolio size**: What's the starting point?

4. **Annual contribution rate**: What can be added each year (401k, IRA, taxable accounts, etc.)?

5. **The gap**: Use a compound interest calculator to see if current trajectory reaches the target by the target retirement age. This turns "I should save more" into "I need to close a specific gap."

6. **The levers**: Increase contributions, increase return (portfolio mix), reduce target expenses (lifestyle), or adjust retirement age.

### Common Patterns and Traps

**Abstract goals without numbers**: Any financial goal that doesn't have a specific number and date attached is aspirational noise, not a real goal. Convert every financial goal to math.

**The sequence problem**: For families with children and aging parents, competing financial demands (college, retirement, supporting parents) can paralyze planning. General principle: retirement before college savings (loans exist for college; they don't exist for retirement). But the specific trade-offs are worth naming explicitly.

**The "I'll figure it out later" trap**: At 48, the compounding window for retirement savings is meaningful but not infinite. The difference between starting aggressive savings at 48 vs. 53 is significant. This is worth making concrete.

**Insurance as an afterthought**: Term life, disability insurance, and umbrella liability are unsexy but critical for families. They belong in a goals review.

### Domain-Specific Goal Examples

- 10-year: "Portfolio at $X (on track for retirement at 62); all college savings funded; home paid off"
- 3-year OKR Objective: "Dramatically accelerate retirement readiness and establish full family financial security"
- Annual KR: "Max 401k contributions ($23k) + IRA ($7k) = $30k/year invested, Jan-Dec"
- Annual KR: "Emergency fund at 9 months of expenses by Q3"
- Habit: "Every Friday I review my financial dashboard for 10 minutes" (identity: "I am a disciplined steward of my family's financial future")

## 👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Family & Relationships

**Why this domain is easy to under-invest**: Family feels stable precisely when it's being taken care of — which can create a false sense that no active investment is needed. The pattern of "I'll be more present when work calms down" is one of the most common sources of regret people report in later life.

**Core dimensions**:
- Romantic partnership (if applicable) — quality, investment, growth
- Parenting — presence, relationships, the kind of parent you're becoming
- Extended family and community ties
- Friendships — depth and maintenance of meaningful relationships
- Your own sense of belonging and connection

### Guiding Questions

**Partnership**: "How would you describe the quality and investment level of your primary relationship right now? What would you most want to nurture or change?"

**Parenting**: "What kind of parent do you want to be remembered as? What's the gap between that and today?"

**Kids' timelines**: If there are children, what are the relevant time horizons? (Ages at various milestones, when they leave home, etc.) This shapes urgency.

**Connection gaps**: "Who in your life do you wish you had a deeper relationship with? What gets in the way?"

**The support system question**: "Who are the 5-10 people whose lives and wellbeing are most intertwined with yours? How are those relationships doing?"

### The Large Family Dimension

For parents of multiple children, some additional considerations:

- **Individual relationships vs. family system**: It's easy to manage the family unit while underdeveloping individual relationships with each child. Goals might include specific 1:1 time targets.

- **Bandwidth and margin**: A large family is inherently high-demand. Goals in this domain often need to include protecting margin (energy, time, financial) rather than adding more.

- **The window awareness**: Kids' years at home are finite and non-recoverable. This creates a natural urgency that financial goals, for example, don't have in the same way.

### Common Patterns and Traps

**The relationship maintenance fallacy**: Strong relationships require active investment, not just the absence of conflict. "Things are fine" isn't the same as "we're building something great."

**Quantity substituted for quality**: More family time doesn't automatically mean better family relationships. Goals in this domain benefit from being specific about *what kind* of presence and connection.

**Neglected friendships**: Deep friendships almost universally require intentional maintenance after your 30s. Without active effort, they quietly fade. This is worth naming as a goal.

### Domain-Specific Goal Examples

- 10-year: "Strong, trusting relationships with each of my children as they enter adulthood; a marriage that's gotten stronger over time"
- 3-year OKR Objective: "Deepen individual relationships with each child and protect the marriage through a demanding season"
- Annual KR: "Monthly 1:1 date with each child, consistent throughout the year"
- Annual KR: "Quarterly overnight trip or significant experience with spouse, all 4 completed"
- Habit: "After dinner, phone goes in a drawer for 90 minutes — fully present with family" (identity: "I am someone who shows up for my family")

## 💼 Career & Business Impact

**Why this domain is complex for someone in software/tech**: Career goals have a particularly wide range — from "maximize income" to "do work that matters" to "build something that outlasts me." These can coexist, conflict, or evolve. The goal-setting work here is partly about getting honest about what you actually want from your career, not just what you think you should want.

**Core dimensions**:
- Current role effectiveness and impact
- Professional reputation and relationships
- Skill development and craft evolution
- Income and financial trajectory
- Career direction (where are you headed?)
- Legacy and longer-term contribution

### Guiding Questions

**Current state**: "How aligned is your current work with what you actually care about? What's most energizing? What drains you?"

**Impact**: "What does meaningful work look like for you? What problems are you trying to help solve through your work?"

**Trajectory**: "Where do you see your career in 10 years? Is that where you want it to go, or is there a different path you'd choose if you were designing it?"

**Craft and growth**: "What skills or capabilities do you most want to develop in the next 3 years? What would make you significantly better at what you do?"

**The intersection question**: "What are you uniquely good at, that the market values, that you find meaningful?" (This Venn diagram is worth making explicit — it's where the best career goals come from.)

### Considerations for a Software/Tech Go-to-Market Role

For someone helping businesses apply software to their missions — which often means sales, customer success, solutions engineering, or business development — some domain-specific considerations:

- **Relationship capital**: In this type of work, the network and reputation built over years is a primary career asset. Goals might include explicit relationship-building and maintenance.

- **Domain expertise evolution**: The software landscape evolves fast. Goals around staying current (specific technologies, industry verticals, GTM motions) preserve long-term market value.

- **The build vs. contribute question**: At 48 with 20+ years of career left, it's worth asking whether to double down on the current trajectory or build something more owned (advisory practice, fractional work, a startup, writing/speaking, consulting).

- **Income vs. meaning tension**: If there's a gap between income requirements (large family, retirement gap) and the kind of work that's most meaningful, naming that tension explicitly is more useful than pretending it doesn't exist.

### Common Patterns and Traps

**The treadmill problem**: Career goals often get set by external pressure (promotions, titles, compensation benchmarks) rather than internal clarity. It's worth separating "what does success look like to me" from "what does success look like to my industry/peers."

**Skill complacency**: High performers in a domain can coast on existing skills for years. The skills that built a successful career in the past aren't always the ones that will build it in the future.

**The 'one more year' trap**: For people who are financially motivated to keep earning but emotionally ready for something different, "I'll reassess in one more year" can become "I'll reassess in ten more years."

### Domain-Specific Goal Examples

- 10-year: "Recognized expert in [domain]; have built and developed a team; having significant impact on the companies/missions I work with"
- 3-year OKR Objective: "Establish a clear area of deep expertise and build a strong external reputation"
- Annual KR: "Complete [specific certification/program] and publish 4+ pieces of thought leadership"
- Annual KR: "Have 3 senior relationships in target companies deepened to genuine partnership level"
- Habit: "Every Monday morning I spend 30 minutes reading in my area of expertise" (identity: "I am someone committed to mastery")

## 🌱 Personal Growth

**What this domain is and isn't**: This isn't the catch-all for "things I feel guilty about not doing." It's about the intentional development of capabilities, perspectives, and experiences that make you more fully who you want to be. This includes intellectual development, creative pursuits, spiritual or philosophical practice, physical adventures, and experiences that expand your sense of what's possible.

**Core dimensions**:
- Learning and intellectual development
- Creative expression
- Spiritual or contemplative practice
- Adventure and experiences
- Self-knowledge and psychological growth
- Mentorship (giving and receiving)

### Guiding Questions

**Curiosity**: "What are you most curious about right now? What topics or skills would you explore if you had unlimited time?"

**Creative expression**: "Is there something creative you've always wanted to do or develop? What's been in the way?"

**The un-lived life question**: "Is there something you used to do or be that you've lost touch with? Something that felt like 'the real you' that got squeezed out by responsibilities?"

**Growth edges**: "Where do you feel most stuck, limited, or like you're operating below your potential — not in work terms, but as a person?"

### Common Patterns and Traps

**The 'someday' domain**: Personal growth goals are often the first casualties when life gets busy. Building them into specific habits (rather than aspirations) protects them.

**Confusing busyness with growth**: Reading business books and attending conferences can become a substitute for actual development rather than a catalyst for it. The test: is something different in your thinking or behavior? Or are you just consuming content?

**Age as a cap**: There's a cultural narrative that deep personal development — new skills, new experiences, fundamental change — is for the young. This is false and worth actively countering. Many people report their 50s and 60s as their richest period of growth.

## 🌍 Legacy & Contribution

**Why this domain matters and why it's often neglected**: Legacy thinking isn't just for the elderly or the famous. It's the practice of asking "what am I building that will matter and persist?" — and that question applies to a career, a family, a community, a skill, an organization. For many people, connecting to a sense of legacy is what transforms daily life from maintenance into meaning.

**Core dimensions**:
- The impact you want your career to have beyond your own advancement
- The kind of parent, partner, friend you're building yourself into
- Community or civic contribution
- What you're creating that others will be able to build on
- The stories your kids will tell about you

### Guiding Questions

**The 30-year view**: "What problem in the world are you most troubled by or most motivated to address — even in a small way?"

**Contribution through work**: "What would it look like if your work was more oriented toward genuine contribution versus personal success?"

**The family legacy question**: "What are the values, practices, or memories you most want to transmit to your kids? What are you actively doing to transmit them?"

**The community question**: "Are you more of a consumer or a contributor in your community? What would more contribution look like?"

### Integration with Other Domains

Legacy is often less a separate domain and more the *meaning layer* that connects the others:
- Health legacy: modeling vitality and self-respect for your kids
- Financial legacy: building security that transcends your lifetime
- Career legacy: the impact on the people and organizations you've worked with
- Family legacy: the kind of family culture you're consciously creating

When someone's other domains feel hollow, it's often because this layer is underdeveloped. Legacy thinking is what turns goals from a to-do list into a life.
