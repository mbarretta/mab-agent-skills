# Quarterly Planning Reference

How to decompose quota into goals, plan pipegen activities, and set a realistic bar for the quarter.

---

## The Quota Decomposition Stack

Start with the number and work backwards to daily/weekly activities. Each layer should be grounded in real conversion rates from your own history, not industry benchmarks.

```
Team Quota
    ↓ ÷ Win Rate
= Pipeline Needed (total qualified)
    ↓ - Pipeline on Hand
= Pipeline Gap to Generate
    ↓ ÷ Avg. Pipeline per Activity
= # of SE-Driven Activities Needed
    ↓ ÷ Weeks in Quarter
= Weekly Activity Target
```

**Example:**
- Team quota: $2M
- Win rate: 30% → Pipeline needed: $6.7M (3.3x)
- Pipeline on hand: $4M
- Gap: $2.7M to generate this quarter
- Avg. pipeline per workshop/event: ~$150K (estimate from past quarters)
- Activities needed: ~18 events/workshops
- Weeks in quarter: 12 → ~1.5 activities/week

This math is rough — use it to sense-check whether the plan is plausible, not to manage the team to a fraction.

### When you don't know your conversion rates

If you're new to the role or the company doesn't track these precisely:
1. Use industry proxies as a starting point (win rates for enterprise software: 20-35%; coverage ratios: 3-4x)
2. Build your own tracking starting this quarter so next quarter's planning is grounded in actual data
3. Be conservative — it's better to plan for 3.5x coverage and hit 3x than to plan for 2.5x and find out in week 9 you're short

## Pipeline Coverage: What the Ratios Mean

**Below 2x**: At serious risk. Unless win rates are very high or deals are very large, you're unlikely to hit quota from here. Activate emergency pipegen — pull in every SE, every workshop, every customer event you can.

**2x–2.5x**: Risky. Manageable if win rates are strong and deals are in late stages, but no room for slippage. Weekly pipeline reviews become critical.

**3x–3.5x**: Healthy starting position. Normal churn and losses won't knock you off course. This is the target entry position at quarter start.

**4x+**: Either very healthy or your qualification standards are too loose. Run a qualification audit — are all these deals real? Remove poorly qualified deals from the count for a more honest picture.

### Qualification matters

Coverage ratios mean nothing if the pipeline isn't real. Before trusting a coverage number, ask:
- Has an SE been engaged? (Unengaged deals often stall or lose without warning)
- Is there a clear technical champion who can access the buyer?
- Has a pain been articulated — and does it map to something we solve well?
- Is there a realistic path to a decision this quarter?

Deals that can't answer these questions inflate the coverage number without contributing to attainment.

## Pipegen Activity Planning

### Types of SE-Driven Pipeline Generation

**Workshops** (highest leverage per activity):
- Prospect-facing: Targeted at ICP accounts. Invite-only, technical. Best done with AE to bring in relationships.
- Customer-facing: Expansions / cross-sell with existing customers. Lower sourcing risk, high win probability.
- Partner-facing: With strategic ISVs or GSIs — often unlocks new pipeline relationships.

**Conference Talks**:
- Long lead time (3-6 months for CFP), but creates lasting content and brand credibility
- Target practitioner-level conferences where buyers are in attendance, not just vendor summits
- Best when the topic is a genuine customer problem you solve, not a product pitch

**Technical Content (LinkedIn, blog, newsletter)**:
- Lower effort per piece than events but compounds slowly — takes 3-6 months to see pipeline effects
- Best when SEs write in their own voice about real customer problems they've seen
- Works best when amplified by the AE team (they share, comment, engage)

**1:many Technical Demonstrations**:
- Webinars or recorded demos targeted at ICP prospects
- Lower personalization but can reach a large audience efficiently
- Works well for "awareness-to-interest" stage; less effective for late-stage technical closing

### Planning a Pipegen Calendar

For each quarter, map:
1. What's already confirmed? (Conference talks submitted months ago, recurring events)
2. What needs to be created? (New workshops, new content series)
3. Who owns what? (Don't let everything default to the same 1-2 SEs)
4. What AE partnerships are needed? (Most workshops need AE to provide prospect relationships)
5. What are the success criteria? (# attendees, ICP match, pipeline created downstream)

**Common failure mode:** Planning activities in week 2 that require 6 weeks of prep, or relying on a single high-volume event as the pipegen strategy. Diversify — own the calendar for the full 12 weeks.

### Getting AE Buy-In on SE Pipegen

SE-driven pipegen only works if AEs show up with the right prospects. That requires:
- **Early communication**: Share the calendar with AEs in week 1, not week 6
- **Clear value proposition**: "Here's what the attendee will walk away able to do" not "here's why our product is great"
- **Easy execution**: Make it easy for AEs to invite — provide the invitation copy, the FAQ, the calendar invite. The easier you make it, the more likely they follow through
- **Feedback loop**: Tell AEs when pipeline comes from their attendees. Show them the ROI. That creates momentum.

## Setting Team-Level Goals

### How Many Goals

3-6 goals per quarter is the right range for a team. More than 6 and nothing is prioritized. Fewer than 3 and you're missing important levers.

Distribute across the three areas:
- **Pipeline/Revenue**: 2-3 goals (the number, coverage, technical win rate)
- **Team Development**: 1-2 goals (enablement, individual development)
- **Market/Field**: 1-2 goals (something to build, something to learn)

### Making Goals Specific Enough

The test: could you score this goal at the end of the quarter without ambiguity?

**Too vague:** "Improve the team's competitive positioning"
**Specific enough:** "Build and deliver competitive comparison workshop vs. [Competitor X] to 3+ AE-sponsored prospect groups by end of quarter"

**Too vague:** "Generate more pipeline from SE activities"
**Specific enough:** "$500K in SE-sourced pipeline created by Q3 close, as tracked in CRM by SE source tag"

### Quarter-Opening Alignment

Share the quarter's goals with:
- **Your SE team**: Full transparency on the number, the targets, the plan. SEs who understand the why behind their activities are more motivated and more effective.
- **Your AE counterparts** (especially AE leaders): The pipegen calendar, what you need from them, what they can expect from you.
- **Your manager**: Your 3-5 key commitments for the quarter — short version. Proactively managing up reduces surprise reviews later.

## Mid-Quarter Pivots

At the halfway point (week 6), take a hard look at whether the plan is still realistic:

**Ahead of pace**: Is pipeline strong? Is the team ready? Could you take on a stretch goal or accelerate a build project?

**Behind on pipeline**: The instinct is to panic and do more. The better move: diagnose first. Is it a coverage problem (not enough pipeline) or a conversion problem (pipeline exists but isn't moving)? They require different responses.
- Coverage problem → accelerate pipegen activities, loop in AEs on at-risk deals, consider adding events
- Conversion problem → SE coaching, technical evaluation support, identify and unblock specific stuck deals

**Behind on team development**: Usually means planned enablement was deprioritized by deal support. Protect enablement time explicitly in week 7-12, even if it feels like the wrong trade-off. An unprepared team in Q4 costs more than a slightly smaller Q3 pipeline.

## Quarterly Planning Anti-Patterns

**The hockey stick plan**: Everything heavy at the end of the quarter. Workshops planned for weeks 10-11, content planned for month 3. This never works — things slip, deals that need to close don't have time to progress, and the team burns out.

**Cloning last quarter's plan**: Re-running the same workshops, same content, same structure without asking whether it worked. Review last quarter's pipeline creation rate by activity type before planning this one.

**SE-as-responders plan**: A quarter where SE activities are all reactive (AEs bring in deals, SEs respond) with nothing SE-owned. The team gets stretched thin and pipeline generation is entirely dependent on AE activity. Build in at least 2-3 proactive SE-owned activities per quarter.

**Ignoring capacity**: SEs have deal-support obligations that are often unpredictable. Build a realistic capacity estimate: if each SE spends 60% of time on deal support, what's left for pipegen and development? Don't plan for 100% capacity on strategic work.
