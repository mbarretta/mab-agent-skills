# SE Leader Metrics Reference

What to measure, how to interpret it, and what healthy looks like. Organized into leading indicators (predict future outcomes) and lagging indicators (confirm past outcomes). SE leaders who only track lagging metrics are always reacting; leading metrics give you time to course-correct.

---

## Pipeline Metrics

### Pipeline Coverage Ratio
**What it is:** Total qualified pipeline ÷ quota  
**How to measure:** Count only pipeline that's been through at least an initial technical qualification — not every logged opportunity  
**Healthy range:** 3x–4x at quarter start. Below 3x = at risk. Above 5x = either the quota is low or qualification standards are loose  
**Why it matters:** The single most predictive metric for whether you'll hit quota. A 3x pipeline assumes a ~33% win rate; if your actual win rate is lower, you need more coverage  
**Leading or lagging:** Leading — look at this weekly, not just at QBR

### Pipeline Generated by SE Team
**What it is:** New qualified pipeline that originated from SE-owned activities (workshops, talks, demos, content)  
**How to measure:** Tag pipeline source in CRM as "SE-sourced" or "SE-influenced" at creation. Pick one definition and be consistent — don't conflate sourced (SE initiated) with influenced (SE touched)  
**Healthy range:** Varies by company, but SE teams that generate >20% of pipeline are genuinely contributing to growth, not just supporting AEs  
**Why it matters:** Measures the team's proactive contribution to the business vs. reactive deal support  
**Leading or lagging:** Semi-leading — tracks the output of pipegen activities

### Pipeline Velocity
**What it is:** How fast opportunities move through stages  
**How to measure:** Average days per stage, tracked over rolling 90 days  
**Healthy range:** Depends heavily on deal size and complexity. What matters is the trend — is velocity improving or slowing?  
**Why it matters:** Stalled deals are often a signal of unresolved technical objections or a missing stakeholder. SEs can often unstick them  
**Leading or lagging:** Leading for quota attainment — slow velocity early = at-risk deals later

### Technical Win Rate
**What it is:** % of technical evaluations (POCs, bake-offs, deep evaluations) that result in a commercial win  
**How to measure:** Track evaluations at start, wins at close. Separate from overall win rate — this measures SE execution specifically  
**Healthy range:** 65–80% is strong for most enterprise software. Below 50% suggests either qualification gaps (wrong deals getting to POC) or execution gaps (POCs aren't compelling)  
**Why it matters:** Separates SE performance from AE/pricing/timing factors  
**Leading or lagging:** Lagging — use this for retrospective analysis and coaching, not real-time management

## Pipegen Activity Metrics

These are the most important leading indicators for future pipeline health. They measure the inputs that create pipeline — which means you can course-correct before pipeline suffers.

### Workshop / Event Delivery
**What to track:** # of workshops/events delivered per quarter, by type (prospect-facing, customer-facing, conference), with estimated audience size and IQ (ideal customer profile match)  
**Healthy range:** Varies by team size. A rough target: each SE contributes to 1-2 pipegen activities per month  
**Interpretation:** Volume alone doesn't tell you much — track the quality of audience (ICP match) and whether pipeline gets created within 30-60 days downstream

### Content Output (LinkedIn, Technical Writing, etc.)
**What to track:** Pieces published per month, by SE. Qualitative note on topic coverage  
**Healthy range:** Even 2-3 substantive posts per month per SE creates meaningful compounding reach  
**Interpretation:** Don't obsess over impressions or engagement — track whether content topics align with the quarter's strategic priorities and whether the team is covering the right narratives

### Pipeline Created from SE Activities (Lagging Version)
**What to track:** Opportunities created within 90 days of an SE-owned activity, attributed to that activity  
**Why 90 days:** Workshop → discovery → opportunity creation often takes 4-8 weeks  
**Interpretation:** This is how you know whether the pipegen activities are actually working. Low creation rate suggests either wrong audience, wrong topic, or weak follow-up motion with AEs after events

## Team Health Metrics

### Enablement Coverage
**What it is:** % of SEs who can confidently demo and position [key product area or use case]  
**How to measure:** A simple 3-point self-assessment (1 = not ready, 2 = learning, 3 = ready to lead) per topic, with manager calibration  
**Healthy range:** 100% "ready" on core product; 70%+ on new/emerging areas within 60 days of launch  
**Why it matters:** Uneven enablement means field readiness is unpredictable — some customers get great demos, others get confused SEs  
**How to use it:** Set specific enablement targets per quarter based on what's strategically important (new product launch, new vertical, new competitive threat)

### 1:1 Cadence and Quality
**What to track:** Are you holding 1:1s consistently? Are they substantive vs. status updates?  
**Healthy signal:** Consistent cadence (weekly or biweekly), with notes that track development arcs across multiple sessions, not just tactical items  
**Warning signal:** 1:1s get canceled when things get busy — exactly when they matter most  
**How to use it:** Use Obsidian notes to track open threads across sessions. Before each 1:1, review what was discussed 2-4 sessions ago and follow up on development goals

### Individual Development Goal Progress
**What to track:** For each SE, one meaningful growth area per quarter, with a concrete milestone  
**Examples:** "Able to run a solo enterprise POC by end of quarter"; "Presented at one external event"; "Built and delivered new workshop on [topic]"  
**Healthy signal:** At least one development milestone completed per SE per quarter  
**Why it matters:** SEs who feel they're growing stay; SEs who feel stagnant leave — often right when they've built the expertise you need most

### Retention Signals
**Proactive signals to watch for:**
- Decreased engagement or responsiveness (in 1:1s, team meetings, Slack)
- Asking fewer questions / less curiosity about product roadmap
- Disengaging from pipegen activities they previously owned
- External LinkedIn activity increasing (especially around hiring/job content)
- Talking about compensation or title at peers in other companies

**Note:** By the time someone hands in notice, you're typically 60-90 days behind where the retention conversation should have happened. The signals above are the early warning system.

## Market Intelligence Metrics

These aren't traditional metrics — they're tracking the quality and volume of field intelligence the team is capturing.

### Competitive Encounter Rate
**What to track:** # of deals per quarter where a named competitor was actively involved in the evaluation  
**Why it matters:** Gives you a competitive landscape read that's grounded in real deals, not analyst reports  
**How to use it:** If encounter rate with Competitor X jumps from 2 to 8 in a quarter, that's a signal — build a competitive response, loop in Product, update the team's battle cards

### Product Gap Frequency
**What to track:** # of times per quarter a specific product gap was cited by a customer or prospect as a concern or blocker  
**Categorize by:** Deal-blocking (prevented or slowed a close) vs. Nice-to-have (asked about but didn't impact decision)  
**Why it matters:** Frequency of a product gap in the field is often the most compelling argument for roadmap prioritization — more compelling than a single customer request logged by a CSM

### Objection Pattern Tracking
**What to track:** Top 5-10 technical objections encountered in the quarter, with frequency  
**Categories:** Security/compliance, integration complexity, performance/scale, pricing/packaging (technical confusion), support model  
**How to use it:** High-frequency objections the team isn't handling well = enablement gap. High-frequency objections the team handles well = document the winning approach for everyone

### Readiness Gaps
**What to track:** Topics or scenarios where SEs consistently feel underprepared  
**How to capture:** Brief question at the end of each team meeting or 1:1: "What did you encounter this week that you weren't as ready for as you'd like to be?"  
**Why it matters:** This is the most direct signal for what to build or enable next

## How to Present Field Intelligence to Product and PMM

The SE team's field intelligence is most useful when it's synthesized, not just a list of anecdotes. A good field intelligence report to your Product and PMM partners includes:

**1. Patterns, not incidents**
Don't say "Customer X asked about Y." Say "6 of 12 enterprise conversations this quarter raised Y as a concern before they'd advance to POC."

**2. Deal impact, not just frequency**
Distinguish between noise (common question, rarely blocks anything) and signal (less common but consistently deal-relevant).

**3. A concrete ask**
"We need a one-pager that explains how we handle [security scenario] at an architecture level — something an SE can hand a CISO before a technical deep-dive." is more useful than "customers ask about security."

**4. What you already tried**
If the team has been handling an objection a certain way, say so — and say whether it's working. This saves Product from solving a problem the field has already solved.

**Cadence for sharing:** End-of-quarter field intelligence summary is the minimum. For fast-moving competitive situations, a quick Slack or email to PMM as the pattern emerges is better than waiting for the quarterly summary.
